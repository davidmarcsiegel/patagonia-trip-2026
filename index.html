Imodium (Loperamide)
GI · Anti-Diarrheal
⏰ Take: Only when diarrhea is disruptive — NOT for mild or early cases
Slows gut motility — which means it stops your body from flushing out whatever is causing the problem. For mild diarrhea, let it run its course and focus on hydration (Trioral salts + Liquid I.V.). ⚠️ Save this for genuine need. Consult a doctor if possible before starting. Remote locations means no nearby medical facilities Days 5–12.

🚢 Managing Seasickness — Practical Tips

  • Start Bonine the night before boarding (Apr 5) — don't wait until you feel sick
  • Stay on deck in fresh air — going below to a closed cabin makes it much worse
  • Keep your eyes on the horizon, not on your phone or a book
  • Eat light but don't be on an empty stomach — crackers, bread work well
  • Midship and lower decks have the least motion
  • Ginger chews work surprisingly well as a first-line option
  • The crew does this route constantly — they know exactly what's coming
  • Cape Horn landings are weather-dependent — they won't send you if it's unsafe

What to know · What to see · Where to go

🇦🇷 Argentina — Cultural Basics

Greetings: A single kiss on the right cheek is standard for hello and goodbye — even with strangers in social settings. Men typically shake hands with each other. Don't be surprised if it happens immediately upon meeting someone.


Time: Argentines run late — socially, 30+ minutes late is normal and expected. Dinner doesn't start until 9 or 10pm. Don't be the first to arrive at anything social.


Mate: If someone offers you mate (the national drink — a bitter herbal tea sipped through a metal straw from a shared gourd), accepting is a gesture of friendship. Pass it back to the person in charge after drinking — they refill it for everyone. Only say "gracias" if you're done and don't want more.


Food culture: Argentine beef is world-class — order it. Don't ask for anything well done. Food is generally mild, not spicy. Dulce de leche (caramel-like spread) is everywhere and beloved — don't compare it to caramel.


Safety: Buenos Aires is safe for tourists with normal urban awareness. Keep your bag in front of you at cafés, don't flash expensive electronics, and be alert in crowded markets and bus stations. Recoleta is one of the safest neighborhoods.


Tipping: 10% in restaurants is standard and appreciated. Leave cash. Taxis — round up the fare. Not expected but welcome.


Thumbs up: Can be considered rude — use a wave or nod instead.


Sensitive topics: The Perón era and the Malvinas (Falkland Islands) are emotionally charged for Argentines. Avoid bringing them up. Politics and the economy are things Argentines love to discuss among themselves — not so much with foreign guests.


Ordering steak: If you don't specify, it comes medium to well done. Rare is genuinely hard to get here. Also, Argentines salt everything heavily — if you want less salt, say "no use sal, por favor."


Photography: Always ask before taking close-up photos of people. Do not photograph military personnel, police, or official buildings — your camera can be confiscated.

💵 Money — Critical Tips from OAT

Credit cards in Argentina: Visa is commonly accepted; MasterCard and Amex often are not. Some businesses add a 5–10% surcharge for card use (look for the word "recargo"). You cannot leave a tip on a credit card — always have cash for tips.


Currency exchange: Your Trip Experience Leader will guide you to an exchange office for the best rate. Bring large, crisp, undamaged U.S. bills — ideally $100 bills post-2013. The rate is significantly better for $100s than $20s. Never exchange on the street.


ATMs: PIN must be numeric only — many foreign ATMs have no letters on the keypad. ATMs may only accept local bank cards. Have a backup card.


Chile: Credit cards add a 2–4% surcharge. Generally not accepted in remote locations.

🌿 Chile — Key Notes

Pinochet era: Chile still feels the aftershocks of the brutal 1973–1990 military dictatorship. Sensitive topic — follow your guide's lead if it comes up.


Food to try: Centolla (king crab) in Punta Arenas. Empanadas. Pisco sour — the national cocktail. Note: coffee in restaurants is usually instant; find a café for real coffee.


⚠️ Agricultural warning entering Chile: Chile strictly prohibits bringing in any fruit, nuts, vegetables, seeds, dairy, flowers, or unprocessed meats. Fines up to $200 even for a single apple or pack of peanuts. Declare or discard before landing.


⚠️ No WiFi on the ship: The Ventus Australis has no internet. Satellite phone available for emergencies (~$3/min). Download maps, books, and anything else you need before boarding in Ushuaia.

📍 Buenos Aires — Recoleta Neighborhood

Your hotel (Recoleta Grand) is in one of the city's most walkable, elegant neighborhoods — often called the "Paris of South America." French-style architecture, wide tree-lined boulevards, outdoor cafés, museums, and parks all within easy walking distance.


Recoleta Cemetery — 5 min walk. One of the world's great cemeteries — more like an open-air museum with elaborate mausoleums the size of small chapels. Eva Perón is buried here. Allow 1–2 hours. Free entry. English guided tours Tue/Thu at 11am.


El Ateneo Grand Splendid — 15 min walk. A working bookstore inside a stunning 1919 theater — the stage is a café, the balconies are browsing areas. One of the most beautiful bookstores in the world. Worth a visit even if you don't buy anything.


Floralis Genérica — 10 min walk. A giant steel flower sculpture in a reflecting pool. Its petals open at dawn and close at dusk. Oddly moving.


National Museum of Fine Arts (MNBA) — 10 min walk. Free. European and Argentine works spanning 800+ years. Good option on a hot afternoon.


La Biela Café — right next to the cemetery. Historic café where motor racing champions gathered in the 1950s. Great spot for coffee and people-watching under a massive rubber tree.


Plaza Francia weekend market — if you're there on a weekend, there's an artisan fair in the plaza adjacent to the cemetery. Crafts, art, street performers.


Palermo — 15 min by taxi. Hipper neighborhood with the best restaurant scene, parks, and nightlife. Good dinner destination in the evenings.

📍 Ushuaia — End of the World

The southernmost city in the world, with a dramatic mountain backdrop dropping straight into the Beagle Channel. More of a base than a destination, but worth a wander.


Museo del Fin del Mundo — Small but genuinely interesting museum covering the history of Tierra del Fuego, indigenous Yámana people, and early European explorers. Good rainy afternoon option.


Main street (San Martín) — The pedestrian strip has shops, restaurants, and the tourist vibe is fun to absorb. King crab (centolla) is the local specialty — worth trying at least once.


Beagle Channel views — You'll get plenty of this on the ship, but walking along the waterfront before boarding is a nice way to absorb where you are before departure.

📍 Puerto Natales — Gateway to Torres del Paine

Small, unpretentious Chilean town that exists largely to serve trekkers headed to Torres del Paine. Charming in its own right — worth a couple hours of wandering.


Waterfront (costanera) — Walk along the Last Hope Sound with views of the mountains. Especially beautiful at dusk.


Baguales Brewery — Puerto Natales' first microbrewery. Great place to have a pint and talk to fellow travelers about their experiences in the park.


Milodón Cave — 25km out of town. Where a piece of ancient giant ground sloth skin was discovered in the 1890s. Interesting historical site if you have transport.

📍 Punta Arenas — Strait of Magellan

Your ship embarkation point. Historic port city at the southern tip of South America — once a vital stop on the clipper route before the Panama Canal made it redundant.


Plaza Muñoz Gamero — The main square with a monument to Magellan. Local tradition: kiss the foot of the indigenous figure on the statue for good luck.


Cementerio Municipal — Like Recoleta but wilder and more atmospheric. The elaborate tombs of the city's founding families are fascinating.


Museo Regional de Magallanes — Covers the history of the Strait of Magellan, Patagonian settlement, and indigenous cultures. Worth an hour.

🌊 On the Ship — Cultural Notes

Yámana people: The indigenous people of Tierra del Fuego who survived in these extreme conditions for thousands of years — wearing almost no clothing despite the cold, living in canoes. Wulaia Bay was one of their largest settlements. The small museum there is worth your time.


Cape Horn history: Before the Panama Canal, every ship traveling between the Atlantic and Pacific had to round the Horn. Thousands of sailors died here. Standing on the island puts you at one of the most historically significant maritime locations on earth.


Darwin connection: The Beagle Channel is named for the ship Charles Darwin sailed on during his 1831–1836 voyage. He described these exact waters and the Yámana people in detail. Walking through Wulaia Bay is walking in Darwin's footsteps.

🇧🇷 Iguassu Falls — Brazil Side

The Brazilian side of the falls gives the panoramic view — you see the full scale of the horseshoe. The Argentine side (if your tour includes it) lets you get right up to and partially inside the falls. Both are extraordinary.


Devil's Throat (Garganta del Diablo): The main event — a massive U-shaped waterfall where the roar is physical. You will get wet. Bring the waterproof shell.


Wildlife: Coatis (raccoon-like animals) are everywhere and will try to steal your food. Don't feed them. Toucans, butterflies the size of your hand, and capybaras are all possible.


Heat: 90°F+ with high humidity. Start early, stay hydrated (Liquid I.V. days), wear permethrin clothes and repellent from the moment you step outside.
